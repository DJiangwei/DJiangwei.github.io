Markets are forward-looking, but investors are not free from the past. Institutions, political memories, industrial structures, and social expectations all limit what a market can imagine as plausible.

History is useful when it is treated as a discipline of context. It should not become an analogy machine where every new event is forced into an old template. The point is not to say "this is 1970s inflation" or "this is another Asian financial crisis." The point is to ask which constraints, incentives, and policy memories are still alive.

For macro work, this matters because prices often move before the narrative becomes coherent. Historical context helps separate three things: what is genuinely new, what is a familiar institutional reaction, and what is only a market story looking for authority.

That is why this site keeps history, politics, and social economics close to market sources. They do not replace the tape. They make the tape more legible.
