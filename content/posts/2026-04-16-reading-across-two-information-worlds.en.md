The most useful macro work often begins before a forecast. It begins with deciding which information world is allowed to shape the question.

English-language market writing is usually strong on global framing: rates, liquidity, balance-sheet plumbing, positioning, and the language of international capital. Chinese-language material is often closer to the local texture: policy wording, industrial mood, credit stress, property sentiment, and the words people use before they become translated categories.

Neither side is complete. Each one has blind spots that feel obvious only after you spend time in the other.

For me, the point is not to average the two. The point is to compare them. When the same development appears in both worlds with different emphasis, that difference is often the signal. Sometimes the English-language world sees a global asset-pricing problem while the Chinese-language world sees a domestic policy tradeoff. Sometimes the Chinese-language world captures a change in tone before it is visible in global commentary.

East Meridian exists partly to make this input structure visible. It is a place to track what I read, listen to, and revisit, but also a reminder that information edge often comes from translation gaps rather than faster headlines.
